# Philosophy

hstack exists because hackathons have a pile of work that isn't building.

Before you write a line of code, you need to figure out what to build, whether it's worth building, how to scope it for the time you have, which prize track to target, and what the winners of this hackathon actually looked like. After you build, you need to script a demo, structure a pitch, record a video, and write a Devpost submission. All of that is research and process work that eats hours you could spend building.

Most of this knowledge is scattered. How are Devpost submissions formatted? How are demo videos structured? What recording tools do people use? What technologies are trending this week? What do winning hackathon teams actually do differently? What mindset do they walk in with? Every team answers these questions from scratch, every hackathon, by Googling and asking around.

hstack centralizes all of it into Claude Code commands. Instead of spending two hours figuring out how to structure your submission, you run a command. Instead of guessing which prize track fits your idea, you run a command. Instead of wondering if your idea is even demo-able in 24 hours, you run a command that scores it on the dimensions judges actually care about.

The scoring system is the core. Hackathon judges don't care about TAM, revenue models, or unit economics. They care about: can you demo it, is it novel, does it fit the theme, can you build it in time, does it make them lean forward, and can you explain it in one sentence. Every existing tool scores ideas like a VC would. hstack scores them like a hackathon judge would.

This stays current. As new technologies emerge, as new trends take over, as different types of projects start winning, the commands evolve. The research commands pull live data. The scoring dimensions reflect what actually wins, not what won three years ago.

The goal is simple: you walk into a hackathon and spend your time building, not figuring out how to hackathon.
